Air-Conditioning Thermal Control:
PI vs. ON/OFF Analysis

First-principles thermal modelling of an HVAC system, featuring Euler-method simulation, control topology comparison, and energy efficiency analysis in MATLAB.

MATLAB PI Control Thermal Dynamics Euler Method
View on GitHub ↗ Jump to Thermal Model

Overview

Standard residential air conditioning units often rely on simple bang-bang (ON/OFF) control, which leads to temperature oscillations and electrical stress from frequent compressor cycling. This project mathematically models the thermal dynamics of a standard room and simulates the performance difference between a traditional ON/OFF controller and a continuous Proportional-Integral (PI) controller.

1. Thermal System Mathematical Model

The room is modeled as a lumped-parameter thermal system. The rate of change of the room temperature ($T_{room}$) depends on the ambient outdoor temperature ($T_{amb}$), the equivalent thermal resistance of the walls ($R_{th}$), the thermal capacitance of the air mass ($C_{th}$), and the cooling power injected by the AC unit ($Q_{ac}$).

By applying energy conservation, the continuous-time differential equation is:

$$\frac{dT_{room}}{dt} = \frac{1}{R_{th} C_{th}} (T_{amb} - T_{room}) - \frac{1}{C_{th}} Q_{ac}(t)$$

Numerical Simulation (Euler Method)

To simulate this in MATLAB without relying on high-level blocksets, the differential equation was discretized using the first-order forward Euler method with a fixed time step ($\Delta t$):

$$T_{room}[k+1] = T_{room}[k] + \Delta t \left( \frac{T_{amb} - T_{room}[k]}{R_{th} C_{th}} - \frac{Q_{ac}[k]}{C_{th}} \right)$$

2. Control Topologies Evaluated

Hysteresis (ON/OFF) Control

The baseline system utilizes an ON/OFF controller with a predefined deadband ($\pm 1^\circ\text{C}$ around the setpoint) to prevent rapid relay chattering. While simple to implement, this inherently causes a continuous steady-state ripple in room temperature.

PI Continuous Control

For the advanced topology, a PI controller adjusts the cooling effort continuously, mimicking an inverter-driven compressor. The cooling effort $Q_{ac}$ is calculated based on the error $e(t) = T_{set} - T_{room}(t)$:

$$Q_{ac}(t) = K_p \cdot e(t) + K_i \int_{0}^{t} e(\tau) d\tau$$

The discrete-time implementation in the MATLAB script uses an accumulator for the integral term, with anti-windup logic applied to represent the physical maximum cooling capacity of the compressor.

Simulation Results & Energy Analysis

The MATLAB simulations revealed significant differences in both comfort (temperature tracking) and energy consumption. The PI controller achieved zero steady-state error with no overshoot, eliminating the thermal ripple entirely.
